The proposal · in brief

See it in 360°. Paint what you saw.

Following your Seatrade Miami brief for family-friendly Cozumel excursions, we propose A Mayan Family Day: a 3-hour cultural excursion built around a 60-minute interactive 360° dome experience at Mexico's premier immersive cinema, followed by a hands-on watercolor workshop at our family preserve. Each guest paints the Mayan deity or celestial symbol the astronomer just brought to life on the dome — and takes it home.

The format

A 3-hour Mayan family day. Begin with a 60-minute interactive dome experience — a 20-minute archaeoastronomy film, the Mayan calendar wheel narrated live, and Q&A with a professional astronomer. Then a watercolor workshop at Tierra Maya with Cozumel artist María Argüero.

The souvenir

Each guest paints a Mayan deity or celestial symbol they just saw on the 360° dome — the Sun god Kin, the Moon goddess Ixchel, the Mayan calendar wheel, the World Tree, the cosmic turtle — and takes the finished watercolor home. A tangible, personal Mayan memory.

The family hook

Indoor 360° cinema + indoor jungle workshop. Painting works for every age — grandparents, parents, teens, kids all leave with a finished piece. No swimwear, no physical demands, all-weather friendly. Walking distance from the pier.

Every Mayan day begins with the Sun god Kin. Your guests learn his story — and the story of Ixchel, Noh Ek (Venus), the calendar wheel, and the World Tree — first by seeing them in 360° immersive dome. Then they paint what they just saw, and take the painting home.

Begin a short walk from the cruise pier at Mexico's premier 360° immersive cinema. Inside the 12-meter dome, an interactive 60-minute experience: a 20-minute Mayan archaeoastronomy film covering six great Mayan temples — San Gervasio (here on Cozumel), Chichén Itzá, Uxmal, Edzná, Palenque, Bonampak — and the alignments of the Sun, the Moon, and Venus. Then a live professional astronomer walks the dome's Mayan calendar wheel overhead, names the gods, and answers your family's questions. The cosmos breathes; the children look up.

A short transfer along the malecón delivers your family to Tierra Maya, our family's private cultural preserve in the Cozumel jungle. There, María Argüero — a professional Cozumel painter whose work centers on the Mayan cosmos — leads a 75-minute watercolor workshop.

María has prepared six Mayan-themed templates, one for each of the gods and symbols the dome just brought to life. Each guest chooses: the Sun god Kin, the Moon goddess Ixchel, the warrior planet Noh Ek (Venus), the Mayan calendar wheel, the World Tree of the Maya, or the cosmic turtle of creation. No painting experience required. Watercolors, brushes, and Mayan bark paper are all included.

While the paintings dry, a small Mayan-style tasting on the white limestone clearing — mole, fresh tortillas off the comal, fruit, and water.

Then the walk back to the ship. The watercolor goes home in a protective envelope. The Mayan sky comes home in memory.

The day · in two halves

See the cosmos. Paint what you saw.

The two halves of the day are designed to feed each other. What your guests see in 360° in the morning, they paint by hand in the afternoon — same gods, same calendar, same celestial symbols. The astronomer hands the family the story; the artist hands them the brush.

Half one · the 360° dome. A walk from the cruise pier to Mexico's premier 360° immersive cinema (Cozumel's planetarium). Inside, a 60-minute interactive experience: 20 minutes of Mayan Archaeoastronomy — six temples, three celestial bodies, and the Mayan calendar in stunning 360° visuals. Then the dome lights shift, and a live professional astronomer walks the Mayan calendar wheel overhead, names the gods, and takes the family's questions. The deities the family is about to paint — Kin, Ixchel, Noh Ek — come alive on the dome first.

The watercolor goes home with them.

The artist · who leads the workshop

María Argüero.

A professional Cozumel-based painter whose body of work centers on the Mayan cosmos, the sea, and the celestial. María leads workshops at venues across Cozumel and the Riviera Maya. For this excursion she will lead the watercolor workshop at Tierra Maya, exclusively for Celebrity guests on call days.

María paints in watercolor and oil. Her larger acrylic works depict galaxies, the night sky over the Yucatán, Mayan-coded faces, and underwater scenes of sea turtles and seahorses. Her watercolor classes — intimate, slow, and template-based — have been featured at restaurants and cultural centers in Cozumel.

For A Mayan Family Day, María has prepared a custom set of Mayan-archaeoastronomy templates for guests. Each template ties directly to the deities and symbols guests will have just seen in 360° on the dome — the gods, the calendar, the celestial bodies. The templates are designed for guests with no painting experience to finish a piece they are proud of in 75 minutes.

María is bilingual and accustomed to mixed-language groups. She is exclusive to Visit Cozumel for the workshop format described here.

The templates · what guests paint

Six Mayan-cosmos templates.

Each template is a Mayan deity or celestial symbol the family just saw on the dome. María has designed each one for finishing in 75 minutes by a guest with no prior painting experience.

Template What it depicts The story María tells
Kin · the Sun god A stylized Mayan Sun face surrounded by rays. The god the astronomer just narrated on the dome. Beginning and end of all Mayan time.
Ixchel · the Moon goddess A young-and-old goddess face with the white rabbit on the Moon, her emblem. Whose pilgrimage temple stood at San Gervasio, here on Cozumel.
Noh Ek · Venus The warrior planet on its 8-year cycle, painted in a Mayan night sky. Read with terrifying precision at Edzná.
The Mayan calendar wheel The interlocking Tzolk'in + Haab calendars in a circular composition. The 52-year Calendar Round — how the Maya measured time.
The World Tree (Ceiba) The Mayan tree of the cosmos — roots in the underworld, branches in the 13 heavens. Connects the underworld, the Earth, and the sky.
The Cosmic Turtle A sea turtle painted as the Mayan cosmic carrier of creation. The Maya saw the earth as a turtle's back — and the constellation Orion as its three creation stones.
The narrative arc. The most common pick will be Kin — the Sun god. The astronomer narrates Kin's story under the dome in the morning. Then at Tierra Maya, your family paints him.
