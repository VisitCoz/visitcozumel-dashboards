The proposal · in brief

Three stops. Eight templates. One Mayan day.

A 3-hour cultural day for couples and families not looking for a beach day. Begin at the monument of Ixchel, the Mayan Moon goddess. Then paint a Mayan god or temple in watercolor with a Cozumel artist at Tierra Maya. Then step inside a 360° immersive semisphere for a live Mayan-archaeoastronomy experience. Each guest takes home the watercolor painting they made as a learning activity. As guests paint, the artist narrates the story of the deity each guest chose, the temples that honored them, and the way the Mayan calendar measured their cycles. A light Mayan tasting while the paintings dry.

The format

A 3-hour cultural day in three stops. An opportunity at Monumentos al Mestizaje with Ixchel, the Mayan Moon goddess. A 45-minute watercolor workshop at Tierra Maya with a Cozumel artist. A 60-minute interactive experience inside a 360° semisphere with a local astronomer.

The souvenir

Each guest paints a Mayan god or celestial symbol — the Sun god Kin, the Moon goddess Ixchel, Noh Ek (Venus), the Jaguar (Balam), the conch shell, the Mayan calendar wheel, the World Tree, or the cosmic turtle. A tangible, personal souvenir.

The family hook

Painting works for every age — couples, grandparents, parents, teens, kids all leave with a finished piece. Indoor + outdoor; all-weather friendly. Guests also learn the Mayan calendar, which is different from the Western calendar used today — a small but powerful "did-you-know" they take back to their dinner table.

Every Mayan story begins on the earth and ends in the sky. This excursion gives your family three deliberate steps from one to the other — with a watercolor in hand and the Mayan calendar in mind by the time they return.

Stop one · Monumentos al Mestizaje. Begin at Monumentos al Mestizaje on the malecón — the meeting of two cultures, Mayan and Spanish-Mexican. Your guide introduces Ixchel, the Mayan Moon goddess, whose pilgrimage temple at San Gervasio drew Maya seekers from across the peninsula for over a thousand years. A short photo stop before the next stop.

Stop two · Tierra Maya. A short transfer to a jungle preserve. Inside the open-air workshop space, a Cozumel artist — a professional painter — leads a 45-minute watercolor workshop. Each guest picks one of eight Mayan-cosmos templates: the Sun god Kin, the Moon goddess Ixchel, Noh Ek (Venus), Balam (the jaguar who carried the stars on his skin), the Mayan conch shell, the Mayan calendar wheel, the World Tree, or the cosmic turtle. As the family paints, the artist narrates the story of each deity, naming the temples and the celestial cycles. It is a hands-on, story-rich way to learn the Mayan cosmos before the dome brings it to life.

Stop three · the 360° semisphere. A 20-minute video of Mayan Archaeoastronomy that explores six temples (San Gervasio, Chichén Itzá, Uxmal, Edzná, Palenque, Bonampak) and the alignments of the Sun, the Moon, and Venus in stunning 360° visuals. Then the dome lights shift and a live local astronomer walks the Mayan calendar wheel overhead, names the gods your family has just painted, and takes their questions.

The day · in three stops

Meet her. Paint her. See her.

The day is built so each stop earns the next. Guests meet Ixchel as a bronze figure on the malecón. They paint her (or one of seven other Mayan deities) in watercolor at Tierra Maya. They see her named overhead by the local astronomer on the 360° dome. The same gods, three times, in three media. The watercolor goes home.

Stop one · Monumentos al Mestizaje. A bronze monument on the Cozumel malecón that commemorates the meeting of Mayan and Spanish-Mexican cultures. Includes a sculpted likeness of Ixchel, the Mayan Moon goddess, whose pilgrimage temple at San Gervasio — here on Cozumel — drew Maya seekers from across the Yucatán peninsula for over a thousand years. Brief photo stop with the guide's introduction.

Stop two · Tierra Maya · the watercolor workshop. A short transfer to a jungle preserve outside Cozumel. The artist leads a 45-minute watercolor workshop in the open-air clearing. Guests choose from eight Mayan-cosmos templates — designed for guests with no painting experience to finish a piece in 45 minutes. As guests paint, the artist narrates the story of the deity each guest chose, the temples that honored them, and the way the Mayan calendar measured their cycles. A light Mayan tasting while the paintings dry.

Stop three · the 360° semisphere. A short transfer back to town. Inside Mexico's premier immersive 360° cinema, a 60-minute experience: a 20-minute Mayan archaeoastronomy film, then a live local astronomer narrating the Mayan calendar wheel overhead and answering the family's questions. The deities the family just painted — Kin, Ixchel, Noh Ek, Balam — come to life on the dome. The day closes with a short return to the cruise ship.

The artist · who leads the workshop

Our Cozumel artist.

A professional Cozumel-based painter whose body of work centers on the Mayan cosmos, the sea, and the celestial. The artist is exclusive to Visit Cozumel for this excursion format. We've intentionally kept her name out of the public-facing copy to protect both the brand and the artist.

The artist paints in watercolor and oil. Her larger acrylic works depict galaxies, the night sky over the Yucatán, Mayan-coded faces, sea turtles, seahorses, and underwater scenes. Her watercolor classes — intimate, slow, and template-based — have been featured at restaurants and cultural centers in Cozumel.

For Beyond the Mayan Sky, she has prepared a custom set of eight Mayan-archaeoastronomy templates for Celebrity guests. Each template ties directly to a Mayan deity or celestial symbol the family will then see in 360° on the dome. The templates are designed for guests with no painting experience to finish a piece they are proud of in 45 minutes.

As guests paint, the artist narrates each deity's story — Kin and the Sun, Ixchel and the Moon, Noh Ek and Venus, Balam and the stars he carried on his skin. By the time the family reaches the dome, they know the gods by name. The local astronomer's narration lands on prepared ground.

The artist is bilingual (English · Spanish) and accustomed to mixed-language cruise-line groups.

The templates · eight Mayan choices

Eight Mayan-cosmos templates.

Each guest picks one template and paints it in watercolor under the artist's guidance. The artist narrates the deity's story while the family paints — every template is a small Mayan-archaeoastronomy lesson.

Template What it depicts The story the artist tells
Kin · the Sun god A stylized Mayan Sun face surrounded by rays. Beginning and end of all Mayan time.
Ixchel · the Moon goddess A young-and-old goddess face with the white rabbit on the Moon, her emblem. Pilgrimage temple at San Gervasio, here on Cozumel.
Noh Ek · Venus The warrior planet on its 8-year cycle, painted in a Mayan night sky. Read with terrifying precision at Edzná.
Balam · the Jaguar The Mayan jaguar who carries the stars on his skin — the night sky in animal form. Travels through the night sky as a thirstless jaguar.
The Conch Shell The Mayan caracol — ceremonial conch used by priests and astronomers. The same form as Chichén Itzá's observatory tower.
The Mayan Calendar Wheel The interlocking Tzolk'in + Haab calendars in a circular composition. The 52-year Calendar Round — different from the Western calendar.
The World Tree (Ceiba) The Mayan tree of the cosmos — roots in the underworld, branches in the heavens. Connects the underworld, the Earth, and the sky.
The Cosmic Turtle A sea turtle painted as the Mayan cosmic carrier of creation. The Maya saw the earth as a turtle's back — with Orion as its creation stones.
The arc of the day. Each template the family chooses is then named overhead by the local astronomer on the 360° dome at the third stop. The deities greet your family three times in one day — in bronze on the malecón, on paper in their hands, and across the 12-meter dome of Mexico's premier immersive cinema. The watercolor goes home with them. The Mayan calendar — different from the human calendar — stays with them.
