The proposal · in brief

A tangible souvenir. A direct view of the Sun.

Following your Seatrade Miami brief for family-friendly Cozumel excursions, we propose A Mayan Family Day: a 3-hour cultural excursion built around a hands-on watercolor workshop at Tierra Maya and a guided solar-telescope viewing at the planetarium. The day's narrative arc — paint the Mayan Sun god, then look directly at him — ties the two halves into one Mayan story families talk about for years.

The format

A 3-hour Mayan family day. Watercolor workshop at our family's Tierra Maya preserve, led by Cozumel artist María Argüero. Then a guided 16-inch solar telescope viewing at Mexico's most advanced planetarium dome.

The souvenir

Each guest paints a Mayan deity or celestial symbol — the Sun god Kin, the Moon goddess Ixchel, the Mayan calendar wheel, the World Tree, the cosmic turtle — and takes the finished watercolor home. A tangible, personal Cozumel memory.

The family hook

Painting works for every age. Grandparents, parents, teens, kids — everyone leaves with a finished piece. No physical demands, no swimwear, no commitment to a 5-hour day. Indoor + outdoor; all-weather friendly.

Every Mayan day begins with the Sun god Kin. This excursion gives your family a chance to paint him — and then see him directly through a 16-inch telescope, safely magnified into your field of view.

Begin at Tierra Maya, our family's private cultural preserve in the Cozumel jungle. There you meet María Argüero, a professional Cozumel watercolor and oil painter whose work centers on the Mayan cosmos, the sea, and the celestial. María leads a 90-minute watercolor workshop. Each guest chooses a Mayan-themed template — the Sun god Kin, the Moon goddess Ixchel, the warrior planet Noh Ek (Venus), the Mayan calendar wheel, the World Tree of the Maya, or the cosmic turtle of creation — and paints it under her guidance.

No painting experience required. María's templates are designed so every guest, from grandparents to children, finishes a piece they're proud to carry home. Watercolors, brushes, and Mayan-bark-paper sheets are all included.

While the paintings dry, a small Mayan-style tasting — mole, fresh tortillas off the comal, fruit, and water — on the white limestone clearing beneath the trees.

Then a short transfer along the Cozumel malecón to Mexico's premier 360° immersive cinema, walking distance from the cruise pier. The climax is on the rooftop: through a 16-inch solar telescope with a professional safety filter, your family looks directly at Kin, the Sun god — the same god they just painted at Tierra Maya, now magnified into the field of view of their own eyes.

A short walk back to the cruise ship closes the day. The watercolor goes home in a protective envelope.

The day · in two halves

Paint the cosmos. See the Sun.

The two halves of the day are designed to feed each other. What guests paint in the morning, they see in the afternoon — with the same god, the same sky, and a 16-inch telescope between them and Kin himself.

Half one · Tierra Maya watercolor workshop. Ninety minutes with María Argüero in the jungle clearing of our family preserve. María leads each guest through their chosen Mayan-themed watercolor, sharing the story of the deity or symbol as they paint. The Sun god Kin. The Moon goddess Ixchel. Venus, the warrior planet. The World Tree. The Mayan calendar wheel. The cosmic turtle. Each painting is a Mayan-archaeoastronomy story in pigment.

Half two · the rooftop solar telescope. A short transfer along the malecón delivers your family to Mexico's premier 360° immersive cinema, walking distance from the cruise pier. Climb to the rooftop and look at the Sun directly — safely — through a 16-inch solar telescope with a professional filter. The Sun god your family just painted. Inclusive-destination certified; a ground-level alternative scope is available for guests with mobility needs.

The narrative bridge. Painting the Sun god, then seeing him. Painting the Moon goddess, then seeing the daylight sky where she rests. Painting Venus, then learning where in the sky it will rise tonight. The artist and the astronomer hand the family a single story, told in two voices.

The artist · who leads the workshop

María Argüero.

A professional Cozumel-based painter whose body of work centers on the Mayan cosmos, the sea, and the celestial. María leads workshops at venues across Cozumel and the Riviera Maya. For this excursion she will lead the watercolor workshop at Tierra Maya, exclusively for Celebrity guests on call days.

María paints in watercolor and oil. Her larger acrylic works depict galaxies, the night sky over the Yucatán, Mayan-coded faces, and underwater scenes of sea turtles and seahorses. Her watercolor classes — intimate, slow, and template-based — have been featured at restaurants and cultural centers in Cozumel.

For A Mayan Family Day, María has prepared a custom set of Mayan-archaeoastronomy templates for guests. Each template ties directly to the Mayan cosmos — the gods, the calendar, the celestial bodies. The templates are designed for guests with no painting experience to finish a piece they are proud of in 90 minutes.

María is bilingual and accustomed to mixed-language groups. She is exclusive to Visit Cozumel for the workshop format described here.

The templates · what guests paint

Six Mayan-cosmos templates.

Each template is a Mayan deity or celestial symbol that ties directly to the dome of the sky guests will see through the telescope. María has designed each one for finishing in 90 minutes by a guest with no prior painting experience.

Template What it depicts The story María tells
Kin · the Sun god A stylized Mayan Sun face surrounded by rays. The same Sun guests will see through the telescope. Beginning and end of all Mayan time.
Ixchel · the Moon goddess A young-and-old goddess face with the white rabbit on the Moon, her emblem. Whose pilgrimage temple stood at San Gervasio, here on Cozumel.
Noh Ek · Venus The warrior planet on its 8-year cycle, painted in a Mayan night sky. Read with terrifying precision at Edzná.
The Mayan calendar wheel The interlocking Tzolk'in + Haab calendars in a circular composition. The 52-year Calendar Round — how the Maya measured time.
The World Tree (Ceiba) The Mayan tree of the cosmos — roots in the underworld, branches in the 13 heavens. Connects the underworld, the Earth, and the sky.
The Cosmic Turtle A sea turtle painted as the Mayan cosmic carrier of creation. The Maya saw the earth as a turtle's back — and the constellation Orion as its three creation stones.
The narrative arc. The most common pick will be Kin — the Sun god. Guests paint him in the morning, then climb to the rooftop after lunch and look at him directly through the telescope. The painting is the bridge. The watercolor goes home, but the moment the family looked at Kin together is what they remember.
