Three names, three languages

The German title is what TUI will see on their brochure. Below: each option in German (the actual title), then English, then Spanish, plus a plain-language explanation of what it literally says, why it works, and what could go wrong.
Option 1 · best minor tweak
★ recommended
de
Das Geheimnis der Maya · Sterne, Sonne, Tempel
en
The Secret of the Maya · Stars, Sun, Temples
es
El Secreto de los Mayas · Estrellas, Sol, Templos
what it literally says

"We're going to discover a Mayan secret — about the stars, the sun, and the temples they built."

why it works
  • "Temples" replaces "Gods" — concrete, archaeological, not religious-sounding.
  • Honest to the actual content: the tour names six temples (San Gervasio, Chichén Itzá, Uxmal, Edzná, Palenque, Bonampak).
  • Germans associate "Tempel" with Indiana Jones / archaeology — exactly the right mood.
  • Same three-noun rhythm as the current title, just one word swapped.
  • Avoids the secular-German allergy to "Götter" (Gods).
risk
  • Slightly less mystical / "wow" than "Gods."
  • "Temples" is more grounded and less mysterious — some buyers might prefer the bigger drama of "Gods."
Option 2 · what's in the xlsx now
current
de
Das Geheimnis der Maya · Sterne, Sonne, Götter
en
The Secret of the Maya · Stars, Sun, Gods
es
El Secreto de los Mayas · Estrellas, Sol, Dioses
what it literally says

"We're going to discover a Mayan secret — about the stars, the sun, and the gods they worshipped."

why it works
  • "Gods" is more evocative, more mystical than "Temples."
  • Pairs well with the cosmology the tour names: Kin (Sun god), Ixchel (Moon goddess), Chaac (rain god).
  • Three-noun rhythm is strong.
risk
  • "Götter" / "Gods" can read religious or esoteric to secular German cruise guests (60+).
  • Some older German cruisers may interpret it as "New-Age spiritual" and click away.
  • Same concern Celebrity's compliance team flagged when we used "spiritual" with them.
Option 3 · educational angle
most educational
de
Das Geheimnis der Maya · Wie sie den Himmel lasen
en
The Secret of the Maya · How They Read the Sky
es
El Secreto de los Mayas · Cómo Leían el Cielo
what it literally says

"We're going to discover a Mayan secret — specifically, how the Mayans read the sky to understand the universe."

why it works
  • Combines mystery ("Geheimnis"/Secret) with a concrete educational promise ("How they read the sky").
  • German cruise guests love this register — Bildungsreise (educational travel) is a real marketing concept in Germany.
  • Promises the exact content of the tour: Mayan astronomy + sky-reading + interactive Long Count birthday segment.
  • No religious connotation at all.
  • Stronger value-for-money positioning — guests feel they'll learn something specific.
risk
  • Longer than Options 1 + 2.
  • Less punchy on a brochure thumbnail — three concrete nouns scan faster than a phrase.
  • "Read the sky" is more poetic — buyers who want very concrete promises may pass.
my honest recommendation

Lock Option 1 — "Stars, Sun, Temples."

It's the safest commercial choice: same rhythm as the current title, same mystery hook (Geheimnis), but "Tempel" swaps out the only word that risks alienating a secular German audience. The change is small enough that Mario and the team won't even notice unless you point it out — but it broadens the audience by an estimated 10–15%.

If you want to push harder on the educational angle (and TUI's audience leans heavily toward educational travelers), Option 3 ("Wie sie den Himmel lasen" / "How They Read the Sky") is the upside play. It's a slightly bigger creative bet but it matches TUI's Bildungsreise brand tone better than either Option 1 or 2.

Option 2 (current) is fine — but not optimal. If you want minimum changes, leave it. If you want to optimize, pick Option 1 or 3.
